# Measurement Problem

## Definition

The measurement problem is that quantum mechanics describes systems evolving superpositions until measured, but provides no fundamental account of what "measurement" is or why it yields definite outcomes. Different interpretations (Copenhagen, many-worlds, decoherence) address it differently but none is fully satisfactory.

## Why It Matters

Without resolution, quantum mechanics remains philosophically incomplete. If observers are part of the formalism from the start, measurement is no longer an external concept imported late.

## How It Works

### Traditional Approaches
- **Copenhagen**: wavefunction collapse is primitive, measurement is external
- **Many-worlds**: all outcomes occur in branching universes
- **Decoherence**: environment-induced superselection explains apparent classicality but not definite outcomes

### OPH Approach
Records and observer criteria are built into microphysics from the beginning. Definite outcomes are stable record structures that survive synchronization across overlaps. Born probabilities arise from consistency conditions on shared records. Measurement is fundamental, not imported later.

OPH implements this via: finite gauge-register model, patch observables, overlap observables, typed readout packets, mismatch syndromes, repair instruments, record layers, and observer criteria.
